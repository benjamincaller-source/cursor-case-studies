# Brex × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

At Brex, free choice won: more than 70% of engineers picked Cursor, and over 45% of code changes are now fully AI-written—proving adoption follows value, not mandates.

## Blog-Style Narrative

Corporate cards and financial software move fast. So does Brex’s engineering culture—and when it comes to AI tooling, the company refuses to pick winners from the top down. Instead, Brex makes every serious option available and lets engineers decide. Among multiple LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) and competing dev tools (including Cursor versus alternatives like Windsurf), one choice has pulled ahead. As CTO James Reggio puts it, Cursor has become the clear standout.

That approach is harder than it sounds. In regulated, high-stakes domains, it is tempting to standardize early—to reduce risk, simplify procurement, and tell a clean story to leadership. Brex takes a different bet: credibility with engineers is itself a risk reducer. When teams can compare tools side by side and bring their own judgment to bear, the winner is more likely to fit real workflows, not just demo well in a pilot. The result is not a fragmented free-for-all, but a market inside the company where the best option can emerge without a mandate.

That standout status shows up in the numbers. More than 70% of Brex engineers now use Cursor. In an environment where tooling is optional, that level of adoption is a vote of confidence—not a compliance metric. Contract renewals are informed by how engineers actually use products, not by executive mandates. When people can walk away, sustained usage means something. It also reframes the conversation from “did we roll out AI?” to “is AI changing how we ship?”—a much harder and more useful question.

The impact goes beyond headcount on a license. Brex reports meaningful gains in day-to-day development: engineers ship routine work faster, execute large-scale migrations with more velocity, and debug at a higher rate. New hires ramp more quickly, which matters when you’re scaling teams and systems at the same time. Large migrations, in particular, are where assistant tooling can pay compound interest—small accelerations across many files and services add up to quarters reclaimed. Perhaps most strikingly, over 45% of code changes are fully AI-written—a signal that Cursor is not a novelty layer on top of the editor but a serious part of how code gets produced.

Brex is deliberate about what it measures. The company focuses on meaningful outcomes—code quality, architectural improvements, and real engineering leverage—rather than vanity metrics that look good in a slide deck but don’t reflect healthier systems. That discipline pairs naturally with a philosophy of choice: when you measure substance, engineers are more willing to bet their workflow on a tool that actually delivers. Together, the adoption statistic and the share of AI-written changes tell a coherent story: Cursor is widely chosen, and it is chosen for work that lands in version control—not just in chat transcripts.

Reggio’s summary ties the story together. “More than 70% of our engineers now use Cursor,” he says, “and we've seen meaningful gains in day-to-day development, faster execution on large-scale migrations, increased rate of debugging, and even faster onboarding.” In other words, Brex didn’t force a single path. It opened the menu, watched how people ordered, and let the data speak. Cursor didn’t just join the stack—it became the default for most of the team, with AI-written changes now a major slice of how Brex builds.

For organizations wondering how to roll out AI-assisted development without a culture war, Brex offers a template: offer options, measure what matters, and renew contracts based on real usage and outcomes. The tools that earn their place will show up in both the metrics and the day-to-day rhythm of engineering—and at Brex, Cursor has clearly earned its seat at the table.
