# Box × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

At Box—where 800+ developers protect sensitive content for the world’s largest enterprises—Cursor drove 30–50% faster roadmap delivery, 80–90% faster migrations, and a mentorship-led surge in adoption, all chosen first for privacy and security.

## Blog-Style Narrative

**Thirty to fifty percent faster roadmap delivery. Eighty to ninety percent faster migrations.** For a company trusted by the largest organizations with their most sensitive content, those numbers are not vanity metrics—they are proof that AI can accelerate delivery without trading away the governance, privacy, and security standards customers expect from Box.

Box’s challenge was familiar to any enterprise at scale: move faster on roadmaps and technical migrations while maintaining ironclad trust. The company is relied on for **sensitive content and data** at global scale; **security, privacy, and governance** are not talking points—they are product requirements baked into how engineering operates. With **800+ developers**, even small friction compounds. The team could not adopt tools that treated security as an afterthought or that pushed teams into inconsistent, ungoverned workflows. They needed an AI coding partner that fit how Box builds software—and how Box protects customers.

Box **selected Cursor specifically for data privacy and security controls**. That decision mattered because it let the organization experiment and scale without asking teams to choose between velocity and compliance. Once the foundation was in place, the work shifted from “whether we can use AI” to “how we use it well.” A **staff engineer built a standard “AI toolkit” for frontend development using custom Cursor rules**, turning individual tricks into a shared playbook. Instead of every team reinventing prompts and guardrails, Box could encode conventions once and reuse them broadly. In parallel, another group used Cursor to **increase automated test coverage**, a reminder that the goal was not faster churn—it was faster *delivery* with stronger signals about quality.

The impact showed up where migrations often hurt most. On a **React migration**, a proof of concept landed in **three days**; the overall migration ran **80% faster**. A **design system migration was roughly 90% faster**—the kind of calendar compression that changes quarterly planning. **Swaroop Butala, Senior Director of Engineering**, described the human-scale difference: **“Before Cursor, this migration would have taken a developer a full quarter. We are now wrapping up the project in about a week.”** **Aaron Levie, CEO**, framed the strategic choice with unusual clarity for a crowded market: **“It became obvious that Cursor is one of the clear leaders to bet on.”** **Mick Ryan, Staff Engineer**, pointed to the opportunity ahead: **“The world has so many problems to be solved with Cursor. Now is the time to build.”**

Numbers on a slide only matter if the tool survives the workweek. Box reported **85% daily adoption** among developers—evidence that Cursor earned a place in real workflows, not pilot decks. The company also invested in people, not just licenses: a **formal mentorship program** paired power users with newer adopters for **one hour per week**. In **six weeks**, that structure contributed to a **75% increase in usage** and a **25% increase in power users**—a reminder that the best tooling still spreads faster when experts coach beginners on how to apply it.

The takeaway for security-conscious engineering organizations is practical: start with controls that match your risk profile, then compound with standards—rules, toolkits, tests—and programs like mentorship that turn adoption into skill. When those pieces line up, speed and trust can move together—not apart.
