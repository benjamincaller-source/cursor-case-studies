# Stripe × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

Stripe preloaded Cursor on every engineer’s machine, paired it with org-wide rules and grassroots champions, and drove a consistent AI rollout across 3,000+ engineers—lifting developer sentiment to a five-year high while an API famous for 99.9999% uptime stayed the north star.

## Blog-Style Narrative

**When 3,000 engineers adopt AI in lockstep, sentiment doesn’t just move—it can spike.** At Stripe, that is exactly what happened: a consistent AI rollout across more than three thousand engineers, with developer sentiment reaching a five-year high. For a company whose name is synonymous with reliable payments—and whose API is known for 99.9999% uptime—that kind of lift matters. It signals that the tools are not only landing but feeling trustworthy in the hands of the people who ship the product.

Stripe did not treat Cursor as a side experiment tucked in a corner of the org. From day one, Cursor came preloaded on every machine. That choice removed friction at the moment it usually appears: the first hour on a new laptop, the first week in a new repo, the first time someone asks, “What should I use here?” Cursor Rules gave engineers shared codebase context at scale, while teams were still empowered to add their own rules—calibrated at the organization level so local wisdom and global standards could coexist.

The human side of rollout was just as deliberate. New engineers go through a lab that teaches Cursor alongside Stripe’s development environment, so onboarding is not “figure out the toolchain” and then “figure out AI” as separate quests. Adoption spread through a power-user influencer network: lunch-and-learns, parallel agents, prompt tips—formats that respect how engineers actually learn from one another.

Stripe also adapted code review to match a world where LLMs are in the loop. Models flag complex methods or risky files so reviewers know where to spend their attention—turning review from a uniform scan into a guided focus on the places that matter most. The goal is not to automate judgment away, but to aim it—so scarce review time lands on the changes that deserve the closest read.

One finding surprised people who assume junior engineers benefit most from AI first. The longest-tenured engineers saw the biggest productivity gains. Head of Developer Infrastructure Scott MacVicar explained why the pattern showed up: “People who have been at Stripe for a long time have the context in their heads. They're able to clearly see what they want to achieve and direct agents to do it.” That insight is shaping how Stripe thinks about the future—including whether new hires should learn the codebase first before getting full Cursor access, so grounding and acceleration stay in balance.

Through it all, the tone from leadership has been direct. MacVicar said: “My advice for anyone thinking about trying AI coding is, move faster on this.” And the mood on the ground matches the metrics. In his words: “People are really excited about the tools they're getting.”

**The takeaway:** Enterprise AI adoption is not only a licensing decision—it is infrastructure, onboarding, review, and culture in one motion. Stripe paired universal access with shared rules, local flexibility, education, and community-led spread—then measured the outcome in something engineers actually feel. When sentiment hits a five-year high across thousands of builders, you are not just shipping a feature. You are changing how it feels to build.

- Rolling out AI org-wide risks uneven adoption, unclear patterns, and review overload.
- Engineers need shared context across a large, complex codebase.
- New hires need a clear path: tools + environment, not tribal knowledge by accident.

### Slide 3 — Day-one access + shared brain (Cursor Rules)
