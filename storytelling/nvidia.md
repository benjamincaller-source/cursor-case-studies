# NVIDIA × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

NVIDIA scaled committed code threefold with Cursor—without raising bug rates—by pairing semantic reasoning across massive, mixed stacks with custom rules, MCP-powered context, and automation across the full SDLC.

## Blog-Style Narrative

**Three times the committed code. Flat bug rates.**

That is the headline NVIDIA’s engineering leaders share when they describe what changed after Cursor became part of how software gets built across the company. With more than 30,000 daily Cursor users, the shift is not a pilot in a corner of the org—it is woven into product work everywhere Cursor is used in pretty much all product areas and in all aspects of software development, as Wei Luo, VP of Engineering, puts it.

NVIDIA is not a greenfield startup sketching a single monolith. Across three decades, its codebases have grown into vast, interconnected systems spanning varied tech stacks. Teams had already experimented with other AI tools—internal offerings and external vendors—but those efforts rarely translated into meaningful velocity. The missing piece was not another autocomplete. It was deep, reliable reasoning over enormous, heterogeneous repositories.

In that environment, “faster drafts” can actually increase risk: the wrong change in the wrong subsystem creates rework, late-night fixes, and brittle workarounds. What NVIDIA needed was assistance that could hold context the way experienced engineers do—tracing dependencies, respecting conventions, and staying aligned with how software is actually shipped.

Cursor’s semantic understanding of large codebases became the differentiator. Engineers could move from “generate a snippet” to “reason about this system”: how a change propagates, where contracts live, and what must stay consistent when the surface area is huge. That foundation let teams graduate from code generation alone to automating whole slices of the SDLC—code review, testing, debugging, Git flow, CI troubleshooting, and issue tracking—so that the tool met NVIDIA where real work actually happens.

Fabian Theuring, Senior Software Architect, describes how NVIDIA pushed further: custom rules in Cursor to fully automate entire workflows, with MCP servers pulling context from tickets and documentation so the assistant is grounded in the same facts engineers use. We have built a lot of custom rules in Cursor to fully automate entire workflows, he says. That has unlocked Cursor's true potential. The result is not just faster typing; it is faster, more repeatable engineering motion—onboarding that gets new hires productive sooner on unfamiliar code, and senior developers stretching across boundaries (for example, backend engineers taking on frontend work) with less friction.

Perhaps most striking is what did not break. Despite tripling committed code velocity, bug rates stayed flat—a signal that speed came with discipline, not chaos—and code style consistency improved. That combination is the difference between a headline about productivity and a headline engineering leaders can stand behind: more throughput without a quality regression. For an organization where reliability and the quality bar are non-negotiable, that balance matters as much as raw output.

We are using Cursor every day, Theuring adds, and now there's no going back because it has completely changed the way software engineering works.

**Takeaway:** When your constraint is comprehension at scale—not keystrokes—semantic reasoning plus workflow automation turns AI from a novelty into infrastructure. NVIDIA’s story is a playbook: connect the model to real process (rules, MCP, SDLC automation), measure outcomes in commits and quality together, and treat velocity as something you earn without trading away trust in the codebase.
