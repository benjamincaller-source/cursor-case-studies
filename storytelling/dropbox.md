# Dropbox × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

At Dropbox—where thousands of engineers push 300K+ requests per second from their own data centers and a 550K+ file monorepo—Cursor helps teams accept more than a million lines of AI-suggested code every month while keeping velocity in the industry’s upper tier.

## Blog-Style Narrative

**More than a million lines of AI-suggested code land in Dropbox’s codebase every month.** That number is not a vanity metric; it is a signal of how deeply Cursor has woven itself into how one of the world’s largest engineering organizations builds software—and how willing teams are to trust suggestions at volume.

Dropbox operates at a scale that humbles most teams: thousands of engineers, workloads running in company-owned data centers, and a monorepo indexing **550K+ files**. Traffic is relentless—**300K+ requests per second**—so any developer tool has to earn its place beside systems that cannot afford guesswork. The challenge was never “can we adopt AI?” It was whether a tool could keep up with that reality—giving models enough **semantic context** to respect how the codebase is actually structured, not just autocomplete the next line.

Adoption did not start with a top-down mandate. Engineers began experimenting on their own, then shared wins in Slack and internal write-ups. **Ali Dasdan, Dropbox’s CTO**, formalized the momentum by standing up an **“AI champions”** group to amplify what worked and remove friction—so good patterns spread faster than bottlenecks. The inflection point was a company-wide hackathon. The night before the deadline, Dasdan used Cursor for the first time to build a **“smart finder”** project. He finished in about two hours—not because the problem was trivial, but because he could trust the workflow. As he put it: *“I waited until the last minute because I trusted the tool, and it still let me finish the whole thing in about two hours.”*

That experience reframed how leadership thought about AI-assisted development. Dasdan shared the story with peer CTOs, many of whom had not yet seriously tried AI coding tools—a reminder that **speed compounds** when the right people see what is possible on real infrastructure, not in a demo environment.

**Codebase indexing became the backbone of the rollout.** A strong semantic index gave models the structural map they needed to navigate a monorepo of half a million files. In practice, that made the system more than a productivity boost for tenured engineers: **new hires ramp faster**, and leaders get a clearer picture of how code fits together—turning a historically opaque surface into something navigable. Cursor shows up in nearly every step of the SDLC—**writing, reviewing, testing, documentation, and migrations**—and Dropbox tracks **PR throughput and cycle time** in the **upper tier of industry benchmarks**, with **90%+ of engineers** using AI tools weekly.

Dasdan’s philosophy ties it together: *“Speed is the only advantage of any company.”* Dropbox is not treating AI as a plugin; they are, in his words, *“reexamining and redesigning every part of how we build software in the context of AI.”*

**The takeaway:** At elite scale, the winning pattern pairs organic experimentation with deliberate enablement—champions, indexing, and leadership that uses the same tools they ask their teams to trust. When the tool fits the monorepo and the culture, velocity stops being a slogan and becomes measurable: lines accepted, cycles shortened, and engineers who actually use AI tools every week of the year.
