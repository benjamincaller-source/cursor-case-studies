# National Australia Bank (NAB) × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

From months to weeks—or days—National Australia Bank is pairing AI with 6,000+ engineers and technologists to move from requirements to deployment at a pace that would have felt impossible a few years ago.

## Blog-Style Narrative

Banking moves at the speed of trust—and lately, at the speed of software. National Australia Bank (NAB), one of Australia’s Big Four banks, is not treating AI as a novelty. It is treating it as infrastructure for how modern financial services get built: with customers and colleagues in mind, with accountability by design, and with engineering velocity that matches the urgency of the market.

The headline numbers are hard to ignore. NAB reports a **40× leap in generating software requirements** and a **5–6× uplift across development phases**. Those are not incremental gains; they signal a shift in how work enters the pipeline and how quickly teams can iterate from idea to something shippable.

That acceleration shows up in everyday delivery, not just in dashboards. One engineering team added push notifications to the **NAB Trade** app **five times faster** than expected. Another team produced a **demo-ready prototype for a colleague referral app in under ten minutes**. And a merchant onboarding group compressed what would typically be **six months of progressive elaboration into 2.5 days**. These stories matter because they translate abstract “AI productivity” into customer-visible outcomes: features shipped, journeys simplified, and time returned to teams.

Design is part of the same story. UX designer **Kimberley Kam** uses Cursor to turn design concepts into **clickable prototypes in hours instead of days**. Her reaction captures the emotional side of the efficiency gain: *“It’s amazing. I’m able to instantly turn a design concept into clickable prototypes… That’s days of work in just hours!”* Speed without ownership is risky in regulated environments, so Kam also emphasizes accountability: *“Everything it produces has traceability, so you’re still owning everything.”*

Leadership frames the change as both opportunity and responsibility. **Nicholas Di Giuseppe**, Executive for Transformation Engineering, summarizes the early results plainly: *“We’ve seen staggering improvements so far. A 40× leap in generating software requirements and a 5–6× uplift in development phases.”* **Patrick Wright**, Group Executive Technology, connects the trials to end-to-end delivery: *“These trials show how our teams can move exponentially faster—getting from requirements through engineering, development and testing to deployment in weeks or even days instead of months.”* **Pete Steel**, Group Executive Digital, Data & AI, sits alongside that narrative as NAB invests across digital, data, and AI capabilities—reinforcing that this is not a single-tool experiment but part of a broader operating model.

Wright also offers a line that will resonate with any large engineering organization navigating change: *“AI won’t take these jobs, someone using AI will.”* NAB is backing that idea with training through its **tech academy**, an **AI literacy program**, and a **future skills strategy**—because tooling only scales when people know how to use it safely and well.

If there is a unifying takeaway, it is that NAB is aligning speed with governance and human judgment. The bank’s focus spans **four areas: customers, colleagues, software development, and operations**—a reminder that “faster” is valuable only when it improves experiences, strengthens teams, and holds up under scrutiny. In that framing, Cursor and complementary tools like **Amazon Q Developer** are not shortcuts around engineering discipline; they are levers for getting from intent to evidence faster—requirements that are clearer, prototypes that are testable, and delivery that meets the moment.
