# Salesforce × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

At Salesforce—thousands of engineers on a 25+ year codebase—Cursor helped unlock more than 30% velocity acceleration alongside double-digit gains in cycle time, quality, and throughput, with junior engineers leading adoption and one team cutting legacy coverage time by 85%.

## Blog-Style Narrative

**Thirty percent or more.** That is how much Salesforce accelerated engineering velocity after rolling out Cursor—paired with double-digit improvements in cycle time, bug-driven quality, and throughput. For a company whose code spans decades and every kind of system, those numbers are not a footnote; they are a signal that the right AI-native tooling can move the needle at enterprise scale.

The challenge was never ambition. Salesforce runs one of the largest and longest-lived codebases in the industry. Layers of history sit side by side: modern services, long-standing frameworks, and paths carved out under constraints that today’s engineers never experienced firsthand. New hires—especially junior engineers who started their careers during the pandemic—often lacked the steady rhythm of in-person mentoring that helps people map sprawling legacy systems, read between the lines of tribal knowledge, and build intuition for what is safe to change. Senior engineers, meanwhile, needed confidence that a new tool would not add noise to work that was already complex. The question was practical: How do you help people understand existing code faster, ship with more confidence, and keep quality high when the surface area is effectively infinite?

Cursor became the bridge. Junior engineers were among the first to lean in. For many, the product was less about “writing more code” and more about **understanding** code—navigating unfamiliar modules, tracing behavior across layers, and getting unstuck without waiting for the next hallway conversation. That matters when your job is not only to deliver a feature but to internalize how a quarter-century of decisions shows up in the files in front of you. Senior engineers took a familiar path: they proved value on boring, tedious work first—refactors, boilerplate, test scaffolding—then expanded to higher-value tasks once trust was earned. It is a playbook that reduces risk: start where mistakes are easy to spot, then broaden the aperture once the workflow feels dependable.

Adoption followed a pattern Salesforce knows well from other transformations: a small group tries it, the impact becomes undeniable, and the rest of the organization follows. Within months, usage was widespread—not because of slogans, but because teams could point to how day-to-day work changed.

The outcomes matched the energy. Beyond the headline velocity lift, Salesforce tracked three metrics that matter in real production systems: **cycle time**, **quality (measured through bug count)**, and **throughput**—and saw double-digit increases across all three. That combination matters: throughput without quality is debt with a deadline; faster cycle time without tests is a gamble at scale. One team cut time spent on legacy code coverage by **85%**, a striking reduction in work that often blocks teams from moving confidently through older surfaces. Engineers are generating far more unit tests than before—an outcome that compounds over time as systems evolve, refactors land, and regressions become easier to catch before customers do.

Shan Appajodu, SVP of Engineering, put the shift in perspective: *“It’s 0 to 1 in terms of how Cursor has transformed the way our developers use tools to improve quality of the product.”* On the metrics, he was direct: *“We have seen more than double-digit increases in all of these metrics.”* And on what comes next: *“We are just at the starting point of the journey. It’s only going to get better.”*

**Takeaway:** At Salesforce scale, “faster” only matters if it is paired with comprehension and quality. Cursor’s adoption story—juniors first for understanding, seniors first for trust on tedious work, then broad rollout—shows how a disciplined rollout can turn a 25+ year codebase from a liability into a place where measurable velocity and quality move together. If the starting point already looks like this, the next chapters are worth watching closely.
