# Coinbase × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

At Coinbase—1,000+ engineers, 92% adoption—AI-first development cut PR review cycles tenfold, pushed roughly two-fifths of code through AI assistance, and turned Cursor into the company’s preferred IDE after a CEO-led mandate and hands-on engineering leadership.

## Blog-Style Narrative

Cryptocurrency moves fast, but enterprise software adoption rarely keeps pace—unless leadership treats it like a product launch. In 2024, Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong mandated company-wide adoption of AI coding tools. The goal was not novelty; it was velocity with guardrails in an environment where mistakes can be costly and scrutiny is constant. By February 2025, the outcome was concrete: every Coinbase engineer had used Cursor, and for most developers it had become the preferred integrated development environment.

That scale—more than a thousand engineers and adoption in the low nineties—does not happen by sending a memo. Chintan Turakhia, Senior Director of Engineering, spent January through April 2025 working daily in Cursor himself, modeling the behaviors he wanted teams to mirror. Leadership-by-doing turned abstract policy into a visible workflow: how to prompt, how to review AI output, how to structure changes for reviewability, and how to ship safely at speed. When senior leaders live inside the same toolchain as ICs, skepticism has less room to hide—and best practices spread faster than slide decks ever could.

The numbers tell a story operations teams can take to the boardroom. Today, roughly 40% of Coinbase’s code is AI-generated, with a stated goal to reach 50%. Pull-request review time dropped from about 150 hours to about 15—a 10× improvement that reshapes planning, staffing, and release cadence. What used to feel like a calendar problem becomes a same-week problem: less queue time, fewer handoffs, and more predictable delivery. Separately, Coinbase reports a code quality score of 54.6%, compared with a 23.8% industry median—evidence that acceleration did not come at the expense of discipline. Speed without quality is debt; Coinbase is arguing it can have both.

Adoption is never uniform, so Coinbase treated it like data. Turakhia’s team analyzed AI usage patterns and identified cohorts—from minimal users to balanced practitioners to power users—then built playbooks tailored to each. That approach converts enthusiasm into repeatable practice: less guesswork, more shared recipes for safe automation, and clearer expectations for what “good” looks like at different maturity levels. Instead of shaming laggards or idolizing heroes, the org could meet people where they were—and lift the median.

Culture followed capability. The company’s famous “PR speed run”—100 engineers submitting 70 pull requests in fifteen minutes—stressed GitHub’s infrastructure enough to become part of Coinbase lore. At company-wide scale, the pattern held: 800 engineers opened 400 pull requests in thirty minutes. The lesson is not chaos for its own sake; it is that modern platforms must absorb bursts of parallel work when tools collapse cycle time—and that stress tests sometimes reveal where bottlenecks really live.

Behind the headlines sits integration work. Coinbase built an internal Slack bot called Cloudbot, chaining Linear tickets with observability and analytics—Datadog, Sentry, Amplitude, and Snowflake—so engineers spend less time context-switching and more time fixing the right problems. The IDE becomes more powerful when it is not an island: tickets, signals, and customer behavior sit closer to the person writing the fix.

In Armstrong’s words, “By February 2025, every Coinbase engineer had utilized Cursor, which has become the preferred IDE for most of our developers. Single engineers are now refactoring, upgrading, or building new codebases in days instead of months.” That captures the shift from incremental optimization to step-change leverage: one engineer, multi-day outcomes that once required calendar quarters.

Coinbase’s story is a template for regulated, high-stakes engineering: executive mandate, visible sponsorship, cohort-based enablement, measurable quality, and tooling that connects the IDE to the rest of the operational stack. Cursor sits at the center—but the operating model around it is what makes the metrics stick.
