# Upwork × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

From 20% Copilot adoption to nearly universal Cursor use: Upwork’s engineering org is shipping about 50% more code—and most engineers reclaim at least an hour every week.

## Blog-Style Narrative

Upwork runs the world’s work marketplace—matching businesses with independent talent at global scale. Shipping reliable software fast is not a nice-to-have; it is the product. Yet for years, “AI in the IDE” sounded better in slide decks than it felt in daily practice. GitHub Copilot never broke through: only about one in five engineers made it a habit. Then Upwork tried Cursor, starting in a single business unit before rolling it out company-wide. The shift was not incremental—it was a reset in how teams build.

Anton Andreev, Principal Software Engineer, describes the outcome in human terms: Cursor has finally gotten engineers to embrace AI-assisted development as part of their standard workflow, and that is changing how Upwork builds software. Adoption is the story beneath the metrics. Nearly 100% of the engineering team now uses Cursor, a dramatic contrast to the prior ~20% Copilot uptake. When a tool becomes default rather than optional, compounding effects show up everywhere—in reviews, in refactors, in the small fixes that never quite happened before.

The numbers back that narrative. Internal surveys show that 70% of engineers save at least one hour per week—real time reclaimed across roles and seniority. That hour is not abstract productivity theater; it is recurring capacity for design discussions, incident readiness, and the unglamorous work that keeps a marketplace stable. Pull requests tell a complementary story: PR volume is up more than 25%, and the average PR size has more than doubled—over 100% growth. Larger changes can be riskier, but they can also signal that engineers are bundling coherent work instead of fragmenting it across endless micro-edits. Andreev ties those threads together: across roles and levels, the team is seeing an increase of over 25% in PR volume and over 100% in average PR size; together, that means shipping about 50% more code.

What changed? Not ambition—Upwork was already ambitious. What changed is friction. Cursor met engineers where they work: in the editor, in context, with a workflow that feels like assistance rather than interruption. Starting in one unit reduced risk and created proof; expanding company-wide turned a pilot into operating culture. Surveys and engineering telemetry align: people feel the hour saved, and the system sees the throughput.

For leaders, the lesson is practical. AI adoption is not solved by licensing alone. It is solved by fit—how naturally a tool integrates with how teams read code, reason about changes, and ship safely at scale. Procurement can buy access; only workflow fit earns habit. For practitioners, the lesson is equally practical: when nearly everyone adopts the same assistant, reviews get easier to reason about, knowledge spreads faster, and “I’ll clean that up later” becomes “I’ll clean that up in this PR.” The quotes from Andreev are not cheerleading—they are an engineer’s description of a norm shift: assistance is no longer a side experiment; it is part of how software gets written.

Upwork’s marketplace depends on trust between clients and talent. Its engineering culture depends on similar trust—in tools, in process, in each other’s judgment. Cursor did not replace that judgment; it amplified it. The result is measurable: more code shipped, larger cohesive changes, and a majority of engineers buying back time every single week. In a business built on connecting people to opportunity, giving engineers a workflow they will actually use might be the most downstream investment of all.
