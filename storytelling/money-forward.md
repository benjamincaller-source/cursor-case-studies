# Money Forward × Cursor — Storytelling Pack

## One-Liner

Money Forward turned stalled AI-tool pilots into momentum: 1,000+ daily Cursor users, 15–20 hours saved per developer per week, and QA test generation 70% faster—by unifying engineering, product, design, and QA on one agent workspace.

## Blog-Style Narrative

**Hook:** Imagine clawing back nearly a full workday every week—per developer—while cutting QA test creation time by 70%. That is the scale Money Forward, a Japanese fintech with more than 1,000 daily Cursor users, is reporting after betting on Cursor not as another pilot, but as the place where real work happens.

**Challenge:** Money Forward did not lack ambition. Previous engagements with external vendors had promised productivity gains, but adoption stalled. Engineers were not seeing meaningful time savings, so enthusiasm faded. The gap between “AI on a slide deck” and “AI in the repo” stayed wide. Meanwhile, product, design, and QA needed the same clarity and speed engineering craved—without yet another toolchain that only worked for one function.

**How Cursor solved it:** The company’s MEPAR (Engineering Productivity and AI Research) department evaluated multiple tools and landed on Cursor for reasons that map to how teams actually ship: minimal setup, strong performance on large codebases, a unified agent workspace, and visual capabilities—including a built-in browser—that make whole tasks tractable end to end. Engineering went first; then adoption spread to product, design, and QA. The proof came in public: at an engineering all-hands, leaders demonstrated Cursor agents tackling full software engineering workflows from start to finish. Bottoms-up demand from developers followed immediately—engineer adoption jumped 30% within one week.

Individual contributors now point to concrete wins: refactoring iOS service layers, optimizing Rails applications (including one team citing roughly 10× performance gains), managing AWS and GCP with Terraform, and migrating from Vue to React—each area absorbing hours that Cursor now compresses. For QA, the workflow looks like a relay: Jira tickets and Notion docs feed in via MCPs; one agent drafts test cases, a second translates them into Playwright scripts—shrinking test-generation time by about 70%. Product managers use Cursor to extract system relationships from repositories, sketch architecture diagrams, and draft PRDs grounded in real code. Designers prototype against live frontends and pull product analytics without waiting for a handoff queue.

**Results and quotes:** The numbers sit alongside cultural shifts. Aaron Li, Staff Engineer, recalled the inflection point: “We held an engineering all-hands where we showed that Cursor's agents could actually tackle entire software engineering tasks from end-to-end. The bottoms-up demand from developers was immediate.” Xie Lester, Director in Chief Quality Office, described QA’s new rhythm: “The QA team now uses Cursor to analyze incidents, automate test results, and review specs before development.” Ryota Sudo, Product Designer, tied it to craft: “With Cursor, I can access product specs and data myself. That allows me to design with a clearer understanding of how the product actually behaves.” Tran Ba Vinh Son (Group Company CTO), Shoichiro Onishi (PM), and others round out a leadership story that is as much about cross-functional alignment as about lines of code.

**Takeaway:** Money Forward’s story is that productivity tools win when they respect real repositories, real tickets, and real handoffs—and when one workspace can serve engineers, PMs, designers, and QA alike. Cursor did not replace judgment; it shortened the distance between intent and execution, and the organization measured the difference in hours saved, tests shipped, and teams that finally moved together.
